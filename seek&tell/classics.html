Blood in the Snow

The main concept behind the tale “Blood in the Snow” was vampire apocalypse. The idea came while at work talking to a co-worker. We were moving stalk around in the back and just shooting the breeze about different movies and TV shows we had recently watched. I don’t remember how, but we eventually got on the topic of apocalyptic movies and how it felt like the concept of apocalypse is always associated with zombies and how it would be interesting to see some other kind of monster apocalypse. At which point my co-worker shared how there was an okay movie by the name of “Daybreakers” that was based on a vampire plague running rampant. And this was where the gears started spinning in my mind and the idea began to shape.

In order for vampires to roam the land they would have to overcome their greatest weakness, that being the sun. Thus, the eternal winter storm would be the cure to solve this problem. The players start in a small walled off village on the verge of collapse. The village’s mayor had succumbed to his illness leaving his incompetent son in his stead. The towns folk would grow more unrestful as the son continues to act out of fear and lack of experience. Ultimately driving the players to leave the area. But the question was to where? Thus introducing the main plot where the players are required to make decisions based on little knowledge and venture into the unknown and uncover how this nightmare began or if there was anywhere safe from the nightmare of the forever winter.

Another fun and impactful aspect of gameplay was player character blood types. In this world almost every blood time was affected by the curse of the forever winter where despite not having turned humans began to develop a strange craving and need for blood. Those who were pureblood did not suffer the negative effects of this curse. Those afflicted with the curse would each day role a percentile die and if they fell into a certain bracket their body would begin craving blood and without fresh blood of another could potentially lead to humans going into a blood frenzy where they uncontrollably attack another person. This problem could only be partially solved with animal blood. For too much animal blood had dire consequences and would change a man/woman negatively and starving oneself also lead to a grim fate. Lead to a grim world that required the players to come up with unique and sometimes dark solutions to these challenges.

The Tale of Fellfall

Fellfall is a story inspired by a friend’s dream that he shared with me one day. He explained how in his dream he had by chance inherited a kingdom and how this kingdom was in shambles and every person looked for him to solve every problem and how stressful and hellish the experience was. I loved the concept but felt like this alone wouldn’t be enough and how would I make it so multiple people would be called to run the same kingdom. And thus, the fatherless backstory was required. Every player was told that they never met their father as they had left their mother before their birth. This allowed for the classic bard adventurer trope to take effect and when the summons came from the king of the entire kingdom called the players and they were all chosen to be king it made somewhat logical sense.

In this world the players learn that the country has become completely encircled by this dangerous black mist that contained horrifying creatures beyond mortal power that slowly seemed to consume the entire land. Due to the dire situation the kingdom was broken up into several mini kingdoms under the management and rule of the capital and its original ruler King Grundy. The king didn’t really care which of the players was the true heir, but he simply needed a head piece to fill the positions void. Thus, the players are immediately shipped off to the kingdom of Fellfall, their new home.

It quickly becomes apparent the dangers of the land. The castle had no guards, a handful of servants, and was located on the southern edge of the country where the darkness was closest. The city was in complete chaos as two major gangs ran things, the people were on the verge of starvation, and worst of all the citizens had zero respect for the new kings seeing their authority as a joke. As the story progresses the players learn that this world is far from sunshine and rainbows. In this world magic is referred to as Remna which was banned throughout the kingdom without the explicit permission granted by King Gundy himself. Next, they learned that King Grundy didn’t really care for this kingdom and was taxing this kingdom to death, hence the extreme poverty. Lastly, things from the darkness were slowly making their way into the land and are far worse than the players could have imagined. This is just the tip of the ice burg as the plot progresses as royal politics become more prevalent, secrets unfold (such as who the players dad was and how he inherited the kingdom, why he left the messages he did, and the rise of new factions).

Smoke and Mirrors

(TIP): when it comes to campaigns having big secrets or reveals can really set the atmosphere and help players keep engaged and see the importance of doing so. I one time ran a cyberpunk like campaign where the players worked for this secret organization whose job is to uncover paranormal secrets and uncover the unknown. During the fifth session of them working on a particular interesting case they begin to unravel an earth-shattering secret about the city. They learn that a college kid had an ancient artifact that allowed him to see into the future, but it drove the wearer mad causing the players to have a hard time deciphering what was truly in the future and what was false. Ultimately the mans predictions come true and the city they had been adventuring and living in was a lie. They had been living on a large space platform above the actual planet and where the real plot lay. I wouldn’t recommend this large shift in every campaign but for this scenario it revitalized the campaign and gave the players a flesh new feeling and an aww dropping experience.

You may breath the World but they Lead it

(TIP) Remember as a dungeon master that facilitating is the role you play not the story writer. Though you have written out the plot of an amazing adventure, its npcs, key items, and all those other juicy details about the world. It would be wise to remember that your players are always going to do the unexpected. For me personally, I have found that being able to improv as one of my most vital skills. Being able to roll with the punches while keeping the story seamless like it all fell together perfectly despite your players’ free will is the mark of good story weaving. Help guide your players but never force them.
